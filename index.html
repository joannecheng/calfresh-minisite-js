Who actually receives food stamps?

A FRESH look at the facts.

People of all backgrounds receive food assistance, but the vast majority of them were already struggling to make ends meet when they were hit with an a job loss or reduction in hours, or an unexpected expense, often due to illness or disability. We’ve taken a deep dive into our applicant data, and have gathered their stories to provide a more accurate picture of who receives food assistance in America.

At Code for America, we run GetCalFresh.org, which helps 20,000+ households apply for food assistance every month. We know that even people who are experiencing hunger pains have to overcome stigma in order to seek out help. Despite the hardships that they have recently faced, many of our users still feel compelled to describe the reluctance, often shame, they feel in asking for help. They might mention how they waited too long to apply, that they have never asked for help before, or that they hope to be off of food assistance as quickly as possible.

The stigma our users experience arises from inaccurate myths. Myths about food assistance have a deep resonance in American culture. They only need an occasional sensational anecdote to persist. As stories, these myths tend to be more memorable than statistics or research findings. This site presents stories in the service of helping us to recall the facts. It showcases real, representative stories of people seeking and receiving assistance alongside pertinent and rigorous research findings.

  • 7 in 10 SNAP beneficiaries are part of a household with children
  • 1 in 3 are households with seniors or people with disabilities
  • The average benefit is $134 per person per month
  • More than 1 in 2 working age, non-disabled SNAP recipients has a job

The vast majority of our users’ stories are united by two very commonplace themes: precariousness and an unexpected expense or loss of income.footnote The stories first presented on this site describe the precarious financial lives of many Californians. Once cost of living is taken into account, California is the state with the highest poverty rate.footnote The second set of stories describes common shocks or adverse events that lead people to apply for food assistance. Coupled with this vulnerability, an illness, injury, loss of a job, a rent increase, or even a car breakdown might be enough to force a household to begin making choices between food and other necessities. By weaving real-life stories together with solid research, our hope is to begin to replace myths with lived realities in the popular imagination.

[Expandable box / image] What is SNAP? What is CalFresh? What determines eligibility? SNAP is the Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program, which is funded by the federal government and administered by the states. To qualify, individuals must make less than 130% (200% in CA) of the Federal Poverty Rate Pre-tax AND less than 100% of the Poverty Rate after taxes and deductions. For a household of 2, that’s a monthly income of $1,784 gross and $1,372 net. footnote Any citizen or permanent resident who can prove that they qualify can enroll. The program provides a maximum of $192 per month for a household of 1 or $642 per month for a household of 4. Funds are distributed via Electronic Benefit Transfer (EBT) Cards that look and function like a debit card. These funds can only be used at approved stores for food items, such as bread, cereal, fruit, vegetables, fish, meat, and dairy. Recipients cannot purchase alcohol, tobacco, or any non-food items with SNAP money.footnote 1, footnote 2 The program was renamed in each state due to stigma. In California, it’s known as CalFresh.
Myth Fact
People on food assistance don’t want to work For example:
  • 61% of Americans above the poverty level and 41% of Americans below the poverty level believe that welfare benefits encourage people to stay poor (LA Times / AEI, 2016).
  • 36% of all Americans think that those on welfare programs would rather stay on welfare than earn their own living (LA Times / AEI, 2016).
  • People who are below the poverty level are much more likely to say that it’s very hard for the poor to find jobs, compared to those above the poverty level (71% vs. 51%, (LA Times / AEI, 2016)).
Most households on food assistance have a job, but their pay is too low or their hours are unpredictable.
  • Two-thirds of people who receive SNAP are children, seniors, or people with disabilities.

They are a burden to taxpayers Every $5 of SNAP benefits translates into $9 of economic activity. Every billion spent on SNAP supports the equivalent of 8,900 full-time jobs. footnote
Closing the participation gap in California would generate $4.5 billion in additional economic activity and $88 million in local and state taxes. footnote
Nothing can be done / it’s too complicated Government assistance programs do work - they measurably reduce hunger and food insecurity:
  • In 2016, safety net programs kept 7.8% of Californians out of poverty, with CalFresh making the largest dent. Footnote
  • Across the U.S., SNAP reduced the poverty rate in 2015 from 15.4% to 12.8%. Footnote
  • SNAP improves health by reducing food insecurity. Footnote 1, Footnote 2, footnote 3.
People can live comfortably on food assistance The maximum benefit a person can receive is $192per month and can only be used at certain grocery stores on certain items. You can’t buy items like alcohol, tobacco or pet food with food stamp or CalFresh benefits. footnote
They might be taking advantage of the system Financial incentives to states only penalize the wrongful issuance of benefits, not the wrongful denial of benefits, which results in extremely low rates of fraud. If you receive too much money because you got a job and didn’t report it, you’re required to pay the government back.
The system is very conservative: in Federal audits, only 3% of SNAP cases have an overpayment, while 26% of denials or terminations involved an error. footnote
The program is inefficient because it is run by the government 93% of Federal SNAP spending pays for food that SNAP beneficiaries can buy.footnote
Undocumented people use food assistance People who are undocumented are not and have never been eligible for food assistance. footnote

Precarious situations: many Californians struggle to make ends meet

People who have never been on food assistance often think that they are for people who are somehow different from them. In fact, food assistance are a way of coping with a challenge that almost everyone is familiar with: making ends meet. And, despite its size and economic diversity, California is the fourth most expensive place to live in the country, after Hawaii, Washington DC, and New York 1. Food assistance eligibility is based on the federal poverty line, which is the same across the county. Yet, once cost of living is taken into account, as is done in the Census Bureau’s Supplemental Poverty Measure , California has the highest poverty rate of any state.

Economic and food insecurity in California 2

  • In 2017, 1 in 10 Californians, or 4.1 million people, received SNAP.
  • Nonetheless, 11.4% of Californians still struggle to put food on the table.
  • 19.6% of children and 10.3% of the elderly are below the poverty line
  • In 2016, 86% of Californians receiving food assistance were below the poverty line, which was $24,300 for a family of four. 3

These economic circumstances mean that many people have no buffer if they suddenly incur a large expense or lose income. It is not surprising that the most common dilemma GetCalFresh applicants mention in their applications is not being able to afford food after paying for rent, utilities, and gas or fare to get to work. In their applications for food assistance, about three in five of our applicants report having less than $100 on hand.

In some counties, rising income inequality means that a large share of people won’t be able to keep up with the high cost of basic necessities.

Our income combined is $2,600 but we pay $1,984 in rent and $568 for PG&E and that barely leaves us $50 to eat with for the rest of the entire month. This data tells a story of stress: the typical household in several counties is already struggling to make ends meet. These households have no slack in their incomes - they already cannot keep up with the bare necessities. A single car accident, medical emergency, or unexpected layoff can put a family over their budget for months, often forcing a choice between something as essential as food, housing, or medical care.

Put simply, Californians live in circumstances that make it very likely many people will need to apply for food assistance to make it through unexpected challenges. In fact, the most common sentiment expressed in our applications is the difficulty of making ends, particularly after an experiencing an adverse event. The next two sections explore two of the most common types of shocks: a job loss or reduction in hours and illness or disability.

Many participants are between jobs or looking for more hours

A common shock that leads people who are already struggling to make ends meet to apply for food assistance is a job loss or a reduction in hours. Our applicants who have recently had hours cut or lost a job are looking for a better job. When it comes time to renew food assistance after six months, the most common reason applicants tell us for no longer needing food assistance is that they got a job or a better job.

Unexpected illness or disability

  • Applicants themselves get disabled or ill and can no longer work, but receiving SSI is a long process - often taking up to two years
  • Applicants may need to stay home to take care of sick or disabled family members, thereby reducing their income.
