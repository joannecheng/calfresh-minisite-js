Who actually receives food stamps?

A FRESH look at the facts.

People of all backgrounds receive food assistance, but the vast majority of them were already struggling to make ends meet when they were hit with an a job loss or reduction in hours, or an unexpected expense, often due to illness or disability. We’ve taken a deep dive into our applicant data, and have gathered their stories to provide a more accurate picture of who receives food assistance in America.

The stigma placed on recipients of food assistance is fueled by cultural myths so deeply rooted that they only require an occasional anecdote to maintain their force. Though unsupported by the facts, this stigma matters because it deters people from seeking the assistance that would help them get back on their feet. More research has not seemed to help - for decades, study after study has disproved these myths. In recent years, not only have the raw data to examine these myths been available online, but non-partisan policy groups have repeatedly released clear, rigorous rebuttals of these myths. Yet, stories that capture the imagination tend to have more staying power than statistics. Without the scaffolding of a story, statistics are easy to forget. The purpose of this site is to elevate the stories of real people who apply for and benefit from food assistance. Our users’ stories both echo the research and, we hope, will make it memorable.

In running GetCalFresh.org, which helps 20,000+ households who apply for food assistance every month, we know that even people who are experiencing hunger pains have to overcome stigma in order to seek out help. On our site, users can explain why they are applying for food assistance to their county. Despite the hardships that they have recently faced, many of our users are still compelled to describe the reluctance, often shame, they feel in asking for help. They might mention how they waited too long to apply, that they have never asked for help before, or that they hope to be off of food assistance as quickly as possible. Not only do their stories reveal the harm caused by stigma, their descriptions of their lives put the lie to popular stereotypes. None wanted to apply for food assistance. All are striving to improve their situation.

Stories, not statistics, can make people relatable. We can recognize challenges we’ve faced or that friends or family have faced in stories. In fact, the vast majority of our users’ stories are united by two very commonplace themes: precariousness and an unexpected expense or loss of income. Precariousness is well-known to Californians, given that once cost of living is taken into account, it is the state with the highest poverty rate. Among our users, common adverse life events are often accompanied by a steep expense or loss of income. Illness, injury, the loss of a job, a car breakdown, or a rent increase might be enough to force a household to begin making choices between food and other necessities.

In their stories, our users both grapple with the stigma around food assistance and disprove the myths that underlie it. In order to situate research findings about food assistance in the context of everyday life, we obtained permission from hundreds of users to share their stories. We hope to show just how relatable their struggles are and how food assistance helps them get back on their feet.

Making ends meet

The high cost of living puts many Californians in a precarious situation

People who have never been on food assistance often think that they are for people who are somehow different from them. In fact, food assistance are a way of coping with a challenge that almost everyone is familiar with: making ends meet. And, despite its size and economic diversity, California is the fourth most expensive place to live in the country, after Hawaii, Washington DC, and New York 1. Food assistance eligibility is based on the federal poverty line, which is the same across the county. Yet, once cost of living is taken into account, as is done in the Census Bureau’s Supplemental Poverty Measure , California has the highest poverty rate of any state.

Economic and food insecurity in California 2

  • In 2017, 1 in 10 Californians, or 4.1 million people, received SNAP.
  • Nonetheless, 11.4% of Californians still struggle to put food on the table.
  • 19.6% of children and 10.3% of the elderly are below the poverty line
  • In 2016, 86% of Californians receiving food assistance were below the poverty line, which was $24,300 for a family of four. 3

These economic circumstances mean that many people have no buffer if they suddenly incur a large expense or lose income. It is not surprising that the most common dilemma GetCalFresh applicants mention in their applications is not being able to afford food after paying for rent, utilities, and gas or fare to get to work. In their applications for food assistance, about three in five of our applicants report having less than $100 on hand.

In some counties, rising income inequality means that a large share of people won’t be able to keep up with the high cost of basic necessities.

I’m in need of emergency food assistance due to my husband being on a strict diabetic diet per doctor's orders. my husband is also bedridden and can't do anything without assistance from me. due to all the medical equipment and doctor visits I have no time to do anything else but take care of him 24/7. Our income combined is $2,600 but we pay $1,984 in rent and $568 for PG&E and that barely leaves us $50 to eat with for the rest of the entire month. I am 69 years old and I'm in need of emergency food assistance as soon as possible.
My husband is currently working 2 jobs (1 is full time), but we are still living paycheck to paycheck. I am unable to find work at the moment for two reasons. First: I have severe nerve damage in my neck and shoulder, mostly on my left side, which has resulted in severe migraines for about half the month. I have received a permanent disability award through workers comp for this injury. Second: I have three young children, ages 7, 6, and 4. With their school schedules it is very hard to find any type of work. Also, with their growing appetites any financial help would mean the world to our family.
My bank account balance is -$136.00. All of my credit cards are maxed out and I do not know how I am going to buy food, pay my utilities or put gas in my car, let alone pay the rest of my bills this month. I am job hunting, but I have a child with autism and he only goes to a educational program for 3 hours a day. I am a single mom and have both kids full time.
— Applicant from Humbolt County

Working Parents

Many of these working families have an additional challenge, such as a child with special needs or variable income. A single mother of two children with a negative bank balance in Humboldt county wrote, “All of my credit cards are maxed out and I do not know how I am going to buy food, pay my utilities or put gas in my car, let alone pay the rest of my bills this month. I am job hunting, but I have a child with autism and he only goes to a educational program for 3 hours a day.” In Humboldt county the cost of living for a single parent with one child is $3,000 more than the median income.

A teacher trying to provide for herself and her son wrote

“I am currently in difficult financial bind- never been in a position such as now. Even help for one month would help my son and I immensely. I just started working my regular, consistent hours at the school. I was off during the summer and working our summer daycare for just about 3 hours a day except field trip days (1-2 times per week). I barely got by. I am almost embarrassed to be here, but hey, it happens, and I know I am not alone. First and foremost, my son needs to know that food will always be available.”

Living paycheck to paycheck is like a scheduled anxiety attack. My health and weight have declined greatly since moving out because the only food I can afford for myself is fast food & dollar tree items. I get sick more often due to my poor health. My goal is to be able to purchase more groceries that are healthier & more nourishing."

This data tells a story of stress: the typical household in several counties is already struggling to make ends meet. These households have no slack in their incomes - they already cannot keep up with the bare necessities. A single car accident, medical emergency, or unexpected layoff can put a family over their budget for months, often forcing a choice between something as essential as food, housing, or medical care.

I lost my home in the Tubbs Fire, and because of complications with FEMA not being able to understand that I was renting a room out of a large home with multiple people sharing the same situation as I, I did not receive lodging assistance. All of my money that I did receive from FEMA ended up going to hotel costs. After couch surfing for 6 months, I finally found a friend who had a room opening up. Unfortunately I had spent everything on hotel costs, rental applications, and simply trying to get my life back together. I have nothing left. Not even enough to spare for yet another rental application that will most likely be denied. I need help. I can't even afford to buy myself a new bed. I'm putting myself in debt because I thought now would be a good time to see a dentist after 7 years... I figure no one is going to actually read this anyway, so it's mostly just venting. Maybe I'll get lucky, and whoever's in charge of this case will at least get an understanding of what those of us who didn't own 3 homes before the Tubbs Fire are struggling with.

Put simply, Californians live in circumstances that make it very likely many people will need to apply for food assistance to make it through unexpected challenges. In fact, the most common sentiment expressed in our applications is the difficulty of making ends, particularly after an experiencing an adverse event. The next two sections explore two of the most common types of shocks: a job loss or reduction in hours and illness or disability.

Many participants are between jobs or looking for more hours

A common shock that leads people who are already struggling to make ends meet to apply for food assistance is a job loss or a reduction in hours. Our applicants who have recently had hours cut or lost a job are looking for a better job. When it comes time to renew food assistance after six months, the most common reason applicants tell us for no longer needing food assistance is that they got a job or a better job.

The hiring job asked me to put in my two weeks notice for my past job. I was already training for my new job when they called me and said the position wouldn't work out since they didn't have it in the budget. Now I'm unemployed and applying for jobs. My partner cannot afford to support us on his $400/per 2 week paychecks."

Although some of my schooling is covered through CalVet college fee waiver program, I am struggling to cover extra school fees and books. I am trying to further my future by earning a degree to one day be financially stable and available to help others in need."

Unexpected illness or disability

  • Applicants themselves get disabled or ill and can no longer work, but receiving SSI is a long process - often taking up to two years
  • Applicants may need to stay home to take care of sick or disabled family members, thereby reducing their income.

Getting disability benefits is a slow and difficult process

The doctor is not allowing her to work for four to six weeks and she only has 600 dollars in her bank account."

Each month, my expenses average $75 for major medical insurance, $80 for one prescription medication, $54 for auto insurance, $30 for auto registration, $250 for propane for cooking and heating (I live in a camper). I do not have dental insurance and can't afford to get care for my bad teeth. I really need help for food and other basic necessities."

But that has made it hard to buy food that I’m supposed to be eating. Trying to make $30 last a week for groceries makes it really hard to buy vegetables and fruit. I have gone down two sizes in a month because I have been balancing the cost of my medications against my purchases of groceries."
